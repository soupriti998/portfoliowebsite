Rethinking AI Cooking in Indian Households

Redesigning an AI-powered cooking assistant so it fits the messy, multitasking reality of real Indian kitchens.

Role Product Designer
Duration 9 months
Platform AI Hardware + Touch
Team Product, Eng, Data

Impact at a glance

Key changes after the redesign.

+30% Recipe completion

More people finished the recipes they started.

2× Recipe ratings

24% Flow efficiency

Faster, smoother core cooking flows for everyday dishes.

Context & Problem

This AI-powered cooking assistant promised to take the stress out of daily cooking in Indian households. In reality, the experience often broke down at the exact moments when people needed it most. Users started recipes with enthusiasm but abandoned them halfway, got confused about what the device was doing, or didn’t trust that the outcome would match their expectations.

Despite strong hardware and ambitious AI features, actual recipe completion and repeat usage were lower than expected. Many users reverted to traditional cooking or felt the device demanded too much of their constant attention.

The core friction was clear: the experience didn’t reflect the reality of Indian kitchens—messy hands, parallel tasks, interruptions, family preferences, and inconsistent attention. My goal was to redesign the core cooking experience so it felt like a reliable partner in the kitchen, not another screen demanding attention.

Goals & Success Metrics

  • Increase recipe completion rate (primary metric).
  • Improve recipe ratings (user satisfaction proxy).
  • Reduce time spent feeling “stuck” or confused in key flows.
  • Increase perceived trust and predictability of the device.

Understanding real kitchens, not ideal flows

To bridge the gap between AI ambition and kitchen reality, we moved beyond the lab:

  • Log Analysis: Identified exactly where users were dropping off in multi-step recipes.
  • Contextual Inquiry: Conducted remote and in-home sessions to observe how people juggled the device alongside their gas stove, pressure cookers, and phone calls.
  • Comparative Interviews: Spoke to both power users and those who had churned to understand the "trust break" moments.

Key insights emerged:

  • Only 1 in 2 recipes were completed; most drop-offs happened mid-way when instructions became dense or timing felt off.
  • Users struggled with unclear step boundaries. "Am I supposed to prep now or wait?" was a common frustration.
  • The device felt unpredictable. Users weren’t sure what was happening inside the jar, leading to anxiety about ruined expensive ingredients.
  • The UI assumed total focus. In reality, cooking is social, interrupt-driven, and chaotic.

Design Challenge

“The core challenge was to reconcile a highly capable AI cooking system with the chaotic reality of Indian kitchens. The experience needed to keep users moving confidently through recipes without forcing them to babysit the device.”

Key Design Decisions

01

The "Add to Jar" Phase

Grouped early prep steps into clear, low-friction instructions focused on adding ingredients. Goal: reduce cognitive load at the beginning so users can start cooking quickly without parsing dense text.

02

Device-Led Action Stages

Introduced a clear distinction between user prep and device actions. After tapping “Start”, the device takes over heating and sautéing with explicit visual feedback. This mirrors a human sous-chef: you prep, they cook.

03

Parallel Prep, Not Idle Waiting

Instead of showing a static timer, the UI now prompts users to prepare for upcoming steps (chopping, fetching garnishes) while the device is active. This leverages natural multitasking habits.

04

Explicit Task Card States

Defined high-contrast visual states for “needs your action”, “in progress”, and “done”. This eliminated the "is it stuck?" anxiety that led to recipe abandonment.

05

Surface Continuous Progress

Added persistent progress indicators and always-visible core controls (pause, repeat). This allowed users to quickly recover from interruptions like phone calls or doorbells without losing their place.

Before vs After: A Day in the Kitchen

Previously, a user felt tethered to the device, constantly checking if they had missed a prompt or if the device was still active. In the redesigned flow, the device became a background partner.

A user could start the 'Sauté' phase, walk away to answer the door, and return to find clear instructions for the next prep step already waiting—all while feeling confident that the AI was managing the heat correctly in their absence.

Outcomes & Impact

The redesign led to a significant shift in how users perceived the device. Beyond the immediate jump in recipe completion, qualitative feedback highlighted a restored sense of trust. Users reported feeling that the device was "finally working with them" rather than against them. These results were validated through a mix of cohort analysis and automated interaction logs post-rollout.

What I Learned

  • Context First, UI Second: Understanding the physical environment (messy hands, steam, noise) was more valuable than pixel-perfect screens.
  • Design for Imperfection: Success depended on making the system forgiving and predictable rather than trying to make it "smarter" in isolation.
  • Simplification is Power: The most impactful changes came from clarifying the division of labor between the user and the AI.
  • Collaboration is Key: Grounding ambitious UX ideas in what could actually ship required deep alignment with the data and engineering teams.
